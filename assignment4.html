Modeling in Onshape

Having never used Onshape before, I started with YouTube tutorials to learn the basics. I used this video to learn how to model a propeller: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JSPJQyVPAko, and modified it for number of blades and depth of propeller. Given my mechanical engineering background and my current role in which I use CATIA everyday, I picked up Onshape super quickly. I made the blades 2 mm thick and filleted it to the cylinder body with a 4 mm fillet. The hole cut into the middle of the propeller is diameter 3 mm.

I then improvised and modeled a holder for the fan. I was originally intending it to be a keychain holder, but the final part was a little too thick to attach a keychain. I ended with filleting all the edges, so there would be no sharp inner edges, where I was scared about the fan possibly cracking when I removed support material. The hole in the center is also diameter 3 mm. I did not have a pin yet, but I figured I wanted a 3 mm pin in the center.

At the end I organized the tree into folders, and I renamed the parts so it made sense. Not knowing what pin I would use as a fastener between the two pieces, I modeled a generic pin with a hexagonal cutout for an allen wrench.

I then created the assembly and animated it!

3D Printing

I exported the propeller and keychain holder as stl files. I imported the propeller stil into Dremel, generated support material everywhere, and previewed the layers. I used higher infill because the part was so thin.

Machine Settings:

  • Nozzle Temperature: 130 degrees C
  • Platform Temperature: 35 degrees C
  • Print Speed: 100%
  • 35% infill

The Final Product

While in class during Josh's lecture, I learned that I could purchase screws from him! Knowing that my holes were 3 mm diameter, I opted for the 2 mm shoulder screw and associated heat set insert.

The most challenging part of this assignment was getting the support material off the propeller blades! I was so worried that I would break one of the blades off, but after an hour of trying to delicately cut the support material off with very little progress, I learned that the 2 mm thick blades with the 4 mm fillets were actually pretty hard to break off compared to the support meterial, and eventually I got all the support material off, except for a little bit around the hole, which made it so that extra thickness meant the screw was too short for the assembly.

Not having access to a soldering gun, I just got a hammer and press-fit the heat set insert into the back of the keychain holder.

Given the extra thickness from the support material that I could not scratch off, I used an exacto knife to "countersink" around the hole so that the shoulder screw would sit deeper into the hole. I was then able to screw into the heat set insert in the back!
